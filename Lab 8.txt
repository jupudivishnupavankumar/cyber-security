Objectives of the lab
1. Understand the basic concepts and operations involved in RSA and Diffie-Hellman algorithms.
2. Manually calculate the key exchange and cipher of RSA and Diffie-Hellman algorithms.
3. Understand how to implement RSA encryption and decryption in Python.
4. Understand how to simulate Diffie-Hellman key exchange in Python.
5. Understand the fundamental aspects of the security of RSA and Diffie-Hellman algorithms.

Activity 1: Understand RSA step-by-step
Objective: Manually implement RSA key generation, encryption, and decryption using small primes. Use the lecture slides from the previous week as a guide for this step-by-step process.

Task 1: RSA key generation (manual calculation)
1. Select two small prime numbers, p and q.
2. Calculate n = p * q.
3. Calculate phi(n) = (p - 1) * (q - 1).
4. Select a small public exponent, e.
5. Calculate d = e^-1 mod phi(n). This will be the private key. This involves finding the modular inverse of e.
6. The public key is (e, n) and the private key is (d, n).

Task 2: RSA encrypt message (manual calculation)
1. Select a small message M (represented numerically, e.g., M = 5).
2. Cipher C = M^e mod n.

Task 3: RSA decrypt message (manual calculation)
1. Message is C = M^d mod n.

Activity 2: Understand Diffie-Hellman key exchange
Objective: Manually simulate Diffie-Hellman key exchange by performing group pairing.

Work in pairs (choose any partner). Do not sit with your partner; communicate using Microsoft Teams or another online messenger.
1. Pairing and Communication: Choose a partner to work with. Do not sit next to them, and use Microsoft Teams or another online messenger for communication.

2. Choosing Public Key and Value: Using Microsoft Teams or another online messenger, agree on two public numbers, p and g (where p is a prime number. Ideally, g should be a primitive root modulo p, but for simulation purposes, any number will suffice).

3. Choosing Private Key: Each student must choose one secret number independently. For example, let's say Student 1 chooses 'a' and Student 2 chooses 'b'. You do not need to reveal your secret key to anyone, including your partner.

4. Computing Public Key: Student 1: A = g^a mod p. Student 2: B = g^b mod p.

5. Exchanging Public Keys: Share your public keys with your partner using Microsoft Teams or another online messenger. Do not reveal your secret keys at any stage!

6. Computing Shared Secret Key: Student 1: S = B^a mod p. Student 2: S = A^b mod p.

7. Verification: Compare your secret keys. Both students should arrive at the same S; if not, recalculate to correct it.
